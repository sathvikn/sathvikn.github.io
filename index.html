Research

I'm interested in how humans' understanding of language and meaning can be modeled with a computational perspective, drawing upon tools from fields like natural language processing and machine learning. My research has focused on both behavioral experiments and corpus analyses, and I'm excited to further explore the field.

This spring, I had the privilege of completing an honors thesis under Dr. Stephan Meylan (MIT) and Professors Mahesh Srinivasan and Steve Piantadosi. I analyzed how a contextualized word embeddings from a neural network (BERT) model relations between different meanings (or senses) of the same word, and found that both distances in the vector space and pairwise confusion from a sense classifier are reasonably correlated with human judgements of sense relatedness.

Prior to that, I contributed to three other research projects. I compared the lexical complexity of child-directed and adult-directed speech with a corpus analysis through my current lab, explored expanding FrameNet with word embeddings through the International Computer Science Institute (PI: Dr. Collin Baker), and worked on experimentally comparing models of word recognition with a web-based game of "Telephone" (journal article under review) with the Computational Cognitive Science Lab (PI: Prof. Tom Griffiths).
